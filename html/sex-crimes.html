SEX CRIMES

Often the mere accusation of a sex crime is all that it takes for an individual to be arrested and charged with a sex crime in Oklahoma. That alone is scary. Although a defendant is supposed to be presumed innocent until proven guilty, when it comes to the suspicion of a sex crime, society a person is often presumed to be guilty, and often faces personal, professional, and legal difficulties even prior to prosecution and can damage your reputation, livelihood, and freedom.

RAPE

Rape requires “sexual intercourse involving vaginal or anal penetration” under one or more prescribed circumstances, including force, threat, deception, or the victim’s inability to provide legal consent due to age, intoxication, mental illness or disability, or custodial status. There are only three types of rape charges in Oklahoma: First Degree Rape, Second Degree Rape, and Rape by Instrumentation. However, there are a number of acts that fall into the category of nonconsensual sex: Forcible Rape, Statutory Rape, Date Rape, Spousal Rape, or Marital Rape. Forcible Sodomy is also often associated with rape cases.

FIRST DEGREE RAPE

First Degree Rape or Rape by Instrumentation in the first degree shall include: (1) rape of a child under the age of 14 by an adult 18 years or older; (2) rape of a person unable to give consent because of mental illness or disability; (3) rape of a person who is “unconscious of the nature of the act”; (4) rape accomplished by administering narcotics or anesthetics in order to force the victim to submit; (5) rape accomplished through force or violence or the threat of force or violence; (6) rape by instrumentation resulting in great bodily harm; or (7) rape by instrumentation of a child under the age of 14. This crime is prosecuted as a felony offense and by statute, is punishable by death; however, the United States Supreme Court has ruled that capital punishment is unconstitutional in all but certain homicide cases. First Degree Rape is however punishable by up to life imprisonment. This crime is also considered a violent offense and requires the defendant to serve 85% of their sentence before being eligible for parole.

SECOND DEGREE RAPE

Second Degree Rape is commonly known as statutory rape, including all acts of rape described above that are not specified as first degree rape. For example, second degree rape involves sexual intercourse with a person who is under the age of 16, the legal age of consent in Oklahoma, or who is unable to provide legal consent as a student or a ward of the state. Second Decree Rape is prosecuted as a felony offense and is punishable by imprisonment for not less than one year nor more than fifteen years.

RAPE BY INSTRUMENTATION

Rape by instrumentation is a subcategory that is an act in which any inanimate object or any part of the human body, not amounting to sexual intercourse is used in the carnal knowledge of another person without their consent and penetration of anus or vagina occurs to that person.
This type of carnal knowledge occurs when a victim does not consent to the act, whether or not the victim is married to the perpetrator. If a person agrees to the act, but is under the age of 16, a student committing the act with a school district employee, or a person in the custody of supervision of a government agency, his or her consent cannot be legally granted. This is only considered a first-degree crime if it is committed upon a minor under the age of 14, or if it results in serious bodily injury. In all other circumstances, it is a second-degree crime.

LEWD OR INDECENT PROPOSALS
OR ACTS TO CHILD UNDER 16

It is unlawful for any person to knowingly and intentionally (1) Propose sexual activity to a child under age 16, or a person believed to be under the age of 16. Such proposal can be oral, written, or electronic. This section includes online solicitation of a minor, even when the defendant is “chatting” with a decoy in an undercover sting; (2) Touch, feel, maul, or even “look upon” the body or private parts of a child in an overtly sexual manner; (3) Ask, invite, entice, or persuade a minor under the age of 16 to go to a secret or secluded location for the purpose of engaging in criminal sexual activity; (4) Urinate, defecate, or ejaculate on or in the presence of a minor for the purpose of sexual gratification; (5) Expose one’s body or private parts to a minor; (6) Force a child under the age of 16 to look at pornography or obscene materials; (7) Cause or force a child to witness sex acts performed in his or her presence; or (7) Require a child to touch his or her own private parts or to touch the private parts of another person. This crime is prosecuted as a felony and is punishable by imprisonment for not less than three years nor more than twenty years. However, if the victim is child under the age of 12 at the time the offense was committed, and in such case this crime is punishable by imprisonment for not less than 25 years.

SEXUAL BATTERY

Oklahoma defines sexual battery as the intentional touching, mauling, or feeling of the body of private parts of any person sixteen years of age or older in a lewd and lascivious manner without the consent of that person. This crime is a felony and punishable by up to ten years imprisonment.

INTERNET SEX CRIMES

Internet and computer sex crimes include any illegal activity carried out via ANY electronic or digital means, including cell phones and mobile devices. This can include sexting, child pornography, and solicitating minors online. Other sex crimes such as distributing obscene materials to a minor or lewd or indecent proposals to a minor, may also be perpetrated through internet or mobile technology.
Internet sex crimes are subject to intense prosecution, and public outrage. Any individual accused of being an online predator is subject to public humiliation as well as the violation of his or her liberties in attempt to protect the public.

OKLAHOMA COMPUTER CRIMES ACT

Computer and internet sex crimes of any sexual offense that is carried out by means of electronic technology, including internet connection, cellphone, text messages, mobile devices, computers, computer networks, and electronic storage devices. The Oklahoma Computer Crimes Act prohibits a number of actions involving computer technology, including hacking, fraud, or disrupting a computer, computer system, or network. The Act also expressly prohibits using a computer or network to commit any crime: “No person shall communicate with, store data in, or retrieve data from a computer system or computer network for the purpose of using such access to violate any of the previsions of the Oklahoma Statutes. Any person convicted of violating the provisions of this section shall be guilty of a felony punishable by imprisonment in the State Penitentiary for a term of not more than five (5) years, or by a fine of not more than Five Thousand Dollars ($5,000.00), or by both such imprisonment and fine.
The penalties associated with conviction of violating the Oklahoma Computer Crimes Act are in addition to any penalties of conviction of the primary offense. For sex crimes, these consequences can be staggering, including the possibility of life in prison and/or lifetimes registration as an Oklahoma Sex Offender.

FEDERAL LEGISLATION OF COMPUTER SEX CRIMES

The United States Government has enacted several laws designed to prevent and penalize computer sex crimes and the sexual exploitation of children.
Protection of Children from Sexual Predators Acts of 1998 which amends the federal crime code to set penalties for solicitation of minors and child pornography perpetrated by mail, wire, or computer; which strengthens penalties for repeat sex offenders; and which restricts federal prisoners access to the internet and interactive computer devices.
Cybermolesters Enforcement Act of 2000 which establishes a Federal Internet Crimes Against Children training facility, known as the Innocent Images Online Training Center (IIOTC), to "educate and train local, State, and Federal law enforcement officers and prosecutors who investigate and prosecute Internet and on-line crimes against children."
Child Sex Crimes Wiretapping Act of 1999 which establishes certain sex crimes against children as predicate crimes for the interception of communication. These predicate offenses include child pornography, coercion, and enticement to engage in prostitution or other illegal sexual activity, and the transportation of minors to engage in prostitution or other illegal sexual activity.
Electronic Communications Privacy Act which protects the privacy of certain types of electronic and online communication, but which authorizes the interception of such communication when it may provide evidence of certain criminal offenses, including child pornography, sexual exploitation of children, and other sex crimes against children.

CHILD PORNOGRAPHY

Child Pornography laws make the possession, distribution, or manufacturing of sexually explicit images if minors illegal. By law this is described as any visual depiction or individual image of a minor under the age of 18 engaged in sexual activity with another other than his or her own spouse. Specific prohibited acts include: (1) normal or perverted sexual intercourse; (2) anal sodomy; (3) S&M or bondage; (4) bestiality; (5) oral sex; (6) ejaculation, urination, defection or excretion in the context of sexual conduct; or (7) exposed genitals, buttocks, or breast for the purpose of the sexual stimulation of the viewer. Child pornography can be stored or possessed in a number of formats including computer files, on a cell phone or other mobile device. However, it can also include video, negatives, photos, or even live performance.
Possession of child pornography charges are separated by two categories. The first category is reserved for those individuals who are charged with possession up to 99 images. Videos count against this number at a more significant rate than one image alone.
Simple possession of Child Pornography is prosecuted as a felony and is punishable by imprisonment for not less than three years nor more than twenty years and a fine of up to $10,000.00. It can be argued this is the same penalty range for anyone charged with manufacturing child pornography or distributing child pornography; however, there is another statute that seems to state that manufacturing, distributing, and downloading child pornography or other obscene materials is only punishable by imprisonment for not less than thirty days nor more than ten years.
Aggravated possession of child pornography relates to individuals who possess 100 or more separate materials and is prosecuted as a felony and is punishable by up to life imprisonment and a fine of up to $10,000.00.
Any person convicted of possession of child pornography is labeled as a Level I Sex Offender and is required to register as a sex offender for 15 years upon the first offense.

FEDERAL CHILD PORNOGRAPHY LAWS

The United States Criminal Code defines child pornography as follows:
“Child Pornography” means any visual depiction, including any photograph, film, video, picture, or computer or computer-generated image or picture, whether made or produced by electronic, mechanical, or other means, of sexually explicit conduct, where (A) the production of such visual depiction involves the use of a minor engaging in sexually explicit conduct; (B) such visual depiction is a digital image, computer image, or computer-generated image that is, or is indistinguishable from, that of a minor engaging in sexually explicit conduct; or
(C) such visual depiction has been created, adapted, or modified to appear that an identifiable minor is engaging in sexually explicit conduct. 
18 USC § 2256
As a federal sex crime, the possession, distribution, or production of child pornography is penalized depending on certain variables of the offense. Conviction of first offense – 5 to 20 years in federal prison. Conviction after prior conviction for child pornography, trafficking in children, or certain other crimes related to the sexual abuse of children – 15 to 40 years in prison.
If possession or distribution of child pornography takes place within the state of Oklahoma, the crime is usually prosecuted as a state crime. However, crimes which occur on Indian land fall under federal jurisdiction, and crimes which are perpetrated across state lines, as often happens with online distribution, are typically prosecuted in the United States District Courts.
